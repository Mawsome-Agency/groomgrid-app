## Post 1: Reddit r/doggrooming — "How I handle no-shows as a mobile groomer"

**Title**: How do you all handle no-shows? I finally found a system that works

**Body**:
> I've been mobile grooming for 3 years and no-shows were literally the bane of my existence. I'd drive 30 minutes to a client only to find out they forgot. That's gas money AND a slot I could've filled.
> 
> Here's what finally worked for me:
> 
> 1. **Text reminder 48 hours before** — "Hey! Just confirming Fluffy's appointment on Thursday at 2pm. Reply YES to confirm or let me know if you need to reschedule." I get about 70% response rate on this one.
> 
> 2. **Second text 2 hours before** — This is the one that saves the most time. If they don't confirm, I call. If no answer, I don't drive.
> 
> 3. **Deposit policy** — New clients pay a 25% deposit when booking. I refund it if they show up, keep it if they no-show. Sounds harsh but it cut my no-shows from about 18% to under 5%.
> 
> 4. **Waitlist system** — I keep a short list of clients who want earlier slots. When someone cancels, I text the waitlist. Usually fills the slot within an hour.
> 
> The biggest game-changer was automating the reminder texts. I used to text everyone manually the night before and it took like 30 minutes. Now it happens automatically.
> 
> What systems do you all use? Curious if anyone else has found something that works well.

## Post 2: Facebook Groomer Group — "Mobile grooming pricing tips that actually work"

**Title**: 🐾 Mobile grooming pricing guide — what I wish someone told me when I started

**Body**:
> Seeing a lot of pricing questions lately so I wanted to share what I've learned after 4 years of mobile grooming. Take what's useful, ignore the rest!
> 
> **Base pricing by dog size (my area — adjust for yours):**
> - Small dogs (under 20 lbs): $60-75
> - Medium dogs (20-50 lbs): $75-95  
> - Large dogs (50-80 lbs): $95-120
> - XL/Giant breeds: $120+
> - Cats: $80-150 (yes, really — and worth every penny for the skill required)
> 
> **The mobile premium:** Add 20-40% above salon prices. You're bringing the service TO them. That convenience has a price. Don't apologize for it.
> 
> **Add-ons that are actually worth it:**
> - Teeth brushing: $10-15 (takes 2 min, high perceived value)
> - De-shedding treatment: $25-40 (for double-coated breeds)
> - Blueberry facial: $10 (clients love this for some reason)
> - Nail grinding vs clipping: $10-15 (grinding is gentler, charges more)
> 
> **Pricing mistakes I made:**
> 1. Charging the same as salons — you have van costs, gas, insurance
> 2. Not raising prices yearly — costs go up, your prices should too
> 3. Discounting for "regulars" — loyalty pricing eats your margins fast
> 4. Not charging for difficult dogs — if it takes 2x the time, charge 2x the price
> 
> **The rule of thumb that changed everything:** Calculate your minimum viable rate (all expenses ÷ available appointment slots) and NEVER book below it. Mine is $55/appointment. If I can't charge at least $55, I don't take the booking.
> 
> What pricing strategies work for you? Drop them below! 👇

## Post 4: Reddit — "What I wish I knew before going mobile"

**Title**: I went mobile 3 years ago. Here's what nobody tells you.

**Body**:
> I see a lot of posts about starting mobile grooming so I wanted to share my experience — the stuff I learned the hard way.
> 
> **The good:**
> - Way more money per dog (I charge 30-40% more than my old salon)
> - No salon rent or utilities
> - Set your own schedule (seriously, this is huge)
> - Way less drama than working in a salon
> - Clients LOVE the convenience — they become so loyal
> 
> **The hard truth nobody tells you:**
> - You will spend MORE on your van than you think. Like 2x more. Budget for it.
> - Bathroom breaks are... a situation. Bring a portable toilet solution. You'll thank me.
> - Weather will mess with your schedule more than you expect. Rain = late appointments. Cold = van takes forever to heat up.
> - You ARE the business. No calling in sick. No coverage. If you're down, you're losing money.
> - The isolation is real. You don't have groomer friends to vent to between dogs anymore.
> - Insurance is more expensive than you think. Get commercial auto, not regular.
> - Gas prices WILL affect your profit margin. Build a buffer into your pricing.
> 
> **The things that saved me:**
> - A solid scheduling system from day one. Don't try to do it on paper.
> - Setting up automated text reminders before I had my first 10 clients
> - A clear cancellation/no-show policy posted on my website and in my intake form
> - Building relationships with a couple vet offices for referrals
> 
> Mobile grooming is the best career decision I've made. But go in with your eyes open. Happy to answer questions.

## Post 5: Facebook — "Free client intake form template for groomers"

**Title**: 🐾 FREE Client Intake Form Template — everything you need to ask new clients

**Body**:
> I spent way too long figuring out what to put on my intake form. Here's the template I've refined over 3 years — steal it, adapt it, make it yours!
> 
> **Client Information:**
> - Full name, phone, email, address
> - Preferred contact method (text/call/email)
> - Emergency contact
> 
> **Pet Information:**
> - Pet name, breed, age, weight
> - Spayed/neutered? (Affects behavior and pricing)
> - Vaccination status (REQUIRED — rabies, distemper, bordetella)
> - Known allergies or sensitivities (shampoos, flea treatments, foods)
> - Previous grooming history (where, when, any issues?)
> - Behavior notes (anxious, nippy, needs muzzle, hates feet touched, etc.)
> 
> **Service & Preferences:**
> - Services requested (bath, haircut, nail trim, teeth brushing, etc.)
> - Desired coat length/style (or "groomer's choice")
> - Photo of desired style (I ask them to show me a pic)
> - Any "don'ts" (don't shave, don't trim face, don't express glands — whatever)
> 
> **Authorization:**
> - Permission to provide services as discussed
> - Acknowledgment of risks (matting removal, senior dog handling, etc.)
> - Agreement to cancellation policy (I require 24hr notice)
> - Photo release (optional — I like before/after pics for my IG)
> 
> **Pro tips:**
> - Ask about aggression FIRST, not last. Safety first.
> - "Has your dog bitten a groomer before?" is the most important question you'll ask.
> - Make the vaccine section prominent — it protects you legally.
> - Digital forms are better than paper. Clients fill them out before the appointment.
>
